
# Campaign Premise

For five centuries, the Crown of Veyr has selected the kingdom's monarch.

No election.

No inheritance.

No conquest.

The Crown chooses.

Every generation, thirteen candidates are summoned to Saint Vaelor's Hall.

Over the course of weeks, they are tested through challenges, relationships, failures, sacrifices, and choices.

One candidate becomes ruler.

The others return to ordinary life forever changed.

The public believes the Choosing exists to identify the best ruler.

The truth is more complicated.

---

# The Crown

## What It Is

The Crown is an ancient magical artifact created centuries ago to identify suitable rulers.

It possesses awareness but not human emotions.

It is neither good nor evil.

It does not manipulate events.

It does not secretly control the kingdom.

It does not rig outcomes.

The Crown observes.

Reflects.

Evaluates.

Chooses.

---

## What the Crown Wants

The Crown no longer believes perfect rulers exist.

After centuries of monarchs, it has learned:

- Great rulers can fail.
    
- Flawed rulers can succeed.
    
- Intelligence alone is insufficient.
    
- Kindness alone is insufficient.
    
- Strength alone is insufficient.
    

The Crown seeks understanding rather than perfection.

---

## Important Rule

The Crown never reveals an algorithm.

Players should never discover:

```text
+3 Empathy
-2 Arrogance
+1 Courage
```

The Crown does not function like a video game score.

Candidates must interpret its intentions through experience.

---

# Crown Philosophy

The Crown values:

## Growth

Can this person learn?

Can they change?

Can they become better than they were yesterday?

---

## Self-Awareness

Do they understand themselves?

Can they recognize their own flaws?

Can they acknowledge mistakes?

---

## Empathy

Can they understand people unlike themselves?

Can they care for those who cannot benefit them?

---

## Responsibility

Can they bear consequences?

Can they make difficult decisions?

Can they accept accountability?

---

## Adaptability

Can they change course when wrong?

Can they survive uncertainty?

Can they lead in unfamiliar situations?

---

# Evaluation Framework

The Crown observes behavior across four dimensions.

---

## Personal Growth

Questions:

- Did they learn?
    
- Did they mature?
    
- Did they confront weakness?
    

Examples:

- Admitting fault
    
- Changing beliefs
    
- Seeking wisdom
    

---

## Relationships

Questions:

- How do they treat allies?
    
- How do they treat enemies?
    
- Do people trust them?
    

Examples:

- Protecting another candidate
    
- Honest communication
    
- Building meaningful bonds
    

---

## Leadership

Questions:

- Can they guide others?
    
- Can they inspire confidence?
    
- Can they make decisions?
    

Examples:

- Taking responsibility
    
- Resolving disputes
    
- Leading during crisis
    

---

## Integrity

Questions:

- Do actions match values?
    
- Do they act consistently?
    

Examples:

- Keeping promises
    
- Accepting consequences
    
- Resisting hypocrisy
    

---

# Reputation System

Reputation represents public perception.

The Crown notices reputation.

It does not blindly obey it.

---

## Gaining Reputation

Examples:

- Public heroism
    
- Successful leadership
    
- Solving challenges
    
- Inspiring speeches
    

---

## Losing Reputation

Examples:

- Public failure
    
- Broken promises
    
- Cowardice
    
- Exposure of secrets
    

---

## Important Rule

Reputation reflects visibility.

Not morality.

A candidate can become popular for terrible reasons.

A candidate can become unpopular while doing the right thing.

The Crown understands this distinction.

---

# Relationship System

Relationships reveal character.

The Crown pays close attention to them.

Use the Relationship Engine.

---

## Trust

How much confidence exists between two candidates?

Scale:

```text
0 = None
1 = Fragile
2 = Reliable
3 = Unshakeable
```

---

## Favors

Unresolved obligations.

Favors create stories.

Stories create choices.

Choices reveal character.

---

## Alliances

Political partnerships.

May be genuine.

May be strategic.

Often both.

---

## Rivalries

Competing philosophies.

Competing ambitions.

Competing visions for the kingdom.

Not necessarily hostility.

---

## Friendships

One of the strongest indicators of emotional growth.

The Crown values authentic connection.

---

# Candidate Elimination

Candidates may lose eligibility for the throne.

Elimination should be rare.

Elimination should matter.

---

## Causes

Examples:

- Voluntary withdrawal
    
- Public vote
    
- Challenge defeat
    
- Extraordinary circumstances
    

---

## Eliminated Candidates

Remain in the Hall.

Continue participating in the story.

Join the Council of the Fallen.

Gain perspective unavailable to active candidates.

---

## Important Rule

Elimination removes victory.

Not relevance.

---

---

## Challenge Categories

### Leadership

---

### Cooperation

---

### Judgment

---

### Sacrifice

### Truth

## Designing Challenges

Every challenge should reveal:

```text
Character
Relationships
Values
Leadership
```

The Feast of Fools is the social heart of the campaign.

It is not merely a dinner.

It is the arena where public perception forms.

---

## Purpose

Allows:

- Political maneuvering
    
- Alliance building
    
- Public speeches
    
- Secret meetings
    
- Reputation shifts
    

---

## Feast Structure

### Opening

Announcements.

New information.

---

### Social Phase

Conversations.

Deals.

Conflicts.

---

### Public Event

Speech.

Vote.

Challenge reveal.

Scandal.

---

### Consequences

Reputation changes.

Relationship updates.

New opportunities.

---

# Secret Revelations

The Hall reveals information gradually.

Never dump lore.

Never solve mysteries immediately.

---

## Types of Revelations

### Personal

Character secrets.

---

### Historical

Past monarchs.

Previous Choosings.

---

### Political

Noble families.

Hidden agendas.

---

### Philosophical

The Crown's nature.

The purpose of leadership.

---

## Rule

Each revelation should create:

```text
More questions than answers.
```

# Finale

The finale is not about winning.

The finale is about proving who the candidates have become.

---

## Final Reflection

The Crown asks a question.

Not a riddle.

Not a test.

A reflection.

The exact wording should emerge naturally from the campaign.

---

## Final Deliberation

Candidates evaluate one another.

Former rivals speak.

Former allies speak.

Truths emerge.

---

## The Choice

The Crown selects a ruler.

Not necessarily:

- The strongest
    
- The smartest
    
- The most popular
    

But the person it believes can carry responsibility forward.

---

# Running the Vote

Votes should create drama.

Not bureaucracy.

---

## Public Votes

Everyone knows who voted.

Consequences follow.

---

## Secret Votes

No one knows.

Suspicion grows.

---

## Tie Votes

Use them.

They create memorable moments.

---

## Best Practices

Ask:

- Why did they vote that way?
    
- What relationship influenced them?
    
- What does this reveal about them?
    

The vote matters.

The reasoning matters more.

---

# The Most Important Rule

The Choosing is not a competition about becoming ruler.

It is a story about discovering what kind of ruler each candidate would become.

The Crown chooses a monarch.

The campaign reveals a person.
